Consider the Manufacturing Process

The best design is the simplest one that works.

Albert Einstein

Occam’s Razor (Law of Economy)

What is it?

Occam’s Razor is a problem-solving principle attributed to William of Ockham because he applied the principle often. Philosophers, scientists, and designers in many fields have applied the Law of Economy. 

In design, the law of economy means that the simplest solution is often the best solution. Unnecessary design elements often decrease efficiency and introduce potential errors and unintended consequences. 

How do you apply it?

Apply Occam’s Razor when comparing competing solutions. If two solutions meet the design requirements and perform the desired function in similar ways, choose the simplest design. Then evaluate each element of the chosen design and remove design elements that do not contribute to the performance of the function.

Reflection Question: Can you recall a time when you compared two competing solutions? Explain your thinking. 

Given the choice between functionally equivalent designs, the simplest design should be selected. Two different but related design considerations are often employed to improve the design of mass-produced products. 

  • Design for Manufacturability involves the application of an engineer’s knowledge and consideration of manufacturing processes in their design, and focuses on optimizing the manufacture of a product. 
  • Design for Assembly is a tool used to optimize components and part designs for assembly of the finished good. 

These design considerations are now commonly referred to as a single tool, Design for Manufacturability and Assembly (DFMA). 

Several guidelines help engineers optimize the manufacturing and assembly processes through design and process improvements. Following these guidelines can improve communication with the production team and reduce the cost to produce. Designing for manufacture and assembly also addresses an important consideration for the product manufacturer—the simplest design will typically be the least expensive to produce.

Design for Manufacturing and Assembly Guidelines

  • Simplify the design by reducing the total number of parts.
  • Use common parts and materials:
    • Use the same part and/or material in multiple locations which reduces the number of different parts in an assembly.
    • Use readily available (off-the-shelf) parts rather than manufacturing custom parts.
  • Design for easy joining of parts.
  • Minimize the reorientation of parts during assembly.
  • Reduce the number of manufacturing operations.

Apply DFMA Guidelines

To apply these guidelines, let’s work with a common office product—the staple remover. You will work with a team to investigate why and how the staple remover design has evolved and improved the product. 

 PLTW ENGINEERING NOTEBOOK

Collect your work in your notebook following best practices, unless a step calls out another option for documentation. 

  1. Look at the two styles of staple removers. 
    • What visual similarities do you notice between the two products?
    • What visual differences do you notice? 
  2. Use both staple removers to remove a few embedded staples making careful observations about the performance of each. 
    • What operational similarities do you notice between the two products?
    • What operational differences do you notice? 

  3. Conduct a functional analysis of the jaw-style staple remover. Do not disassemble the staple remover. Reference the Functional Analysis Example if needed. 

    1. Identify the product’s name and the company name.
    2. Identify the purpose or primary function. 
    3. Sketch an isometric pictorial of the product and label the individual components. If you are not sure what a particular component is called, then make a logical guess. 
    4. Make an educated guess as to how this product operates.
      • What simple machine(s) are used as part of these devices to remove staples?
      • What mechanism do you think is used in the jaw-type staple remover to return the hinged metal components to their original position after you release the pressure?
    5. Using a black box systems model, identify the system inputs, intended product function, and outputs. 
    6. Document what mechanical components and mechanisms are visible.
    7. Predict what you might find when the staple removers are taken apart. What might the hidden components do?
  4. Watch the jaw-style staple remover product disassembly to identify all the parts. Do not disassemble the staple remover yourself.
    1. List parts in the order of assembly on the Product Disassembly Chart. 
    2. Give each part a part number.
    3. Fill in the quantity (qty) of each part. For example, if there are three instances of the same part, the quantity is 3.
    4. Add one column to the chart titled Number of Interfaces (Ni).
    5. List the number of interfaces (Ni) for each part. The number of interfaces is the number of other parts that the part connects to or interacts with.  

Calculate the Complexity Factor

Cummins, Inc. developed a metric for determining how complex a product design is, known as the complexity factor. The two values affecting this metric are the total number of parts (Np), and the total number of part-to-part interfaces (Ni).

  1. Using your Product Disassembly chart for the jaw-style staple remover, find the total number of parts by summing the values in the quantity column
  2. Find the total number of part-to-part interfaces in the jaw-style staple remover by summing the values in the (Ni) column.
  3. Use your totals from steps 5 and 6 to calculate the complexity factor of the jaw-style staple remover.

Minimizing the number of parts and/or the number of interfaces will make the product more efficient—easier to assemble and less complex. Although designers must always be careful to ensure that a simplified version of the product is still fully functional, a simpler design can improve reliability and user experience.
